Loneliness

Loneliness is a serious health risk for aging parents

6 min read · Based on U.S. Surgeon General and AARP research

In 2023, the U.S. Surgeon General did something unusual. He issued a formal public health advisory, the same kind of document used for the biggest threats to the country's health, about something most people do not think of as a medical problem at all: loneliness.

Up to 50% higherRisk of dementia in older adults with poor social connection

The advisory laid out the numbers. Poor social connection is linked to a roughly 50% higher risk of dementia in older adults, a 29% higher risk of heart disease, and a 32% higher risk of stroke. Its toll on the body rivals major health risks, and it also raises the risk of early death by nearly 30%.

For an aging parent living alone, this is not abstract. AARP finds that about 40% of adults over 45 report feeling lonely. After a spouse passes, or the last friend moves away, the days get very quiet. And a quiet day, repeated for years, does real damage.

But not any company will do

Here is the part most articles skip. Simply adding more names to the calendar does not reliably fix it. A parent can attend church, keep up with the ward, and see the grandkids on Sunday, and still feel deeply alone the rest of the week. What the research points to is a specific kind of contact: warm, one on one, and initiated by the other person, so the lonely parent does not have to reach out first.

The strongest evidence comes from a randomized trial published in JAMA Psychiatry in 2021. Researchers had trained laypeople make short, empathy focused phone calls to isolated older adults. In just four weeks, loneliness, depression, and anxiety all fell measurably, with a moderate effect on loneliness (Cohen's d of 0.48). The magic was not a program or an app. It was a real voice that called, listened, and cared, on a regular rhythm.

How Elderly helps

Elderly gives your parent exactly that: a warm phone call every day, one they never have to start. It rings their own phone, asks how they are, and actually listens, at a pace that suits an older adult. It cannot replace you, and it is not meant to. It fills the long quiet hours between your visits with a familiar, patient presence, and tells you how they sounded afterward.

Connection is protective. The research is clear that it lowers risk, lifts mood, and keeps the mind engaged. The hard part was never the science. It was building something that reaches a parent every single day, without asking them to learn anything or carry the effort themselves. That is the whole idea behind the daily call.
